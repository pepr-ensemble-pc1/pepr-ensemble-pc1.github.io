## Thesis Title

Joint Actions in a Collaborative Digital Environments: co-gestures and a Sense of Agency

## Abstract

This PhD thesis investigates how groups of three people coordinate actions and experience a sense of agency during collaborative interaction. While research in Cognitive Science and Human–Computer Interaction (HCI) has largely focused on dyadic collaboration, little is known about how coordination and control are distributed in small groups.
The research examines collaborative tasks in which participants jointly manipulate shared objects through coordinated actions such as translation, rotation, and scaling, across both physical and digital environments. Task difficulty is manipulated by varying the level of uncertainty about partners’ goals, allowing the study of how increasing coordination demands affect behavior and subjective experience.
The thesis combines behavioral measures (performance, errors, and completion time) with subjective measures of workload and sense of agency. By linking coordination dynamics with perceived control, this research aims to contribute to theories of joint action and to inform the design of interactive systems that better support multi-user collaboration.
