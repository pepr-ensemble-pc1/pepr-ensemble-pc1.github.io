## Thesis Title

Empathetic storytelling in interactive extended reality

## Abstract

Exhibition lighting design requires collaboration among curators, conservators, scenographers, lighting designers, and technicians. Through interviews and workshops with museum professionals, we identified three challenges in current practice. First, stakeholders describe light differently, from narrative descriptions to technical specifications, yet these perspectives must eventually converge into a unified design. Second, design decisions evolve faster than supporting documentation, causing teams to work from outdated references. Third, selecting appropriate lighting fixtures requires extensive trial and error, slowing the transition from concept to implementation.
We propose a pre-visualization framework that generates accurate lighting effects directly onto photographs of the exhibition space. The system accepts sketches, reference images, and natural language as input, allowing stakeholders to express lighting ideas in familiar terms. By providing a shared visual reference accessible without specialized software or expertise, the system enables collective evaluation before physical installation. This approach aims to reduce miscommunication, accelerates decision-making, and ensures that final lighting configurations reflect input from all collaborating parties.
