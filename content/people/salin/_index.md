## Thesis Title

Environnements Immersifs Partagés pour la Co-Création Artistique en Modalités Hétérogènes

## Abstract

My research investigates how immersive extended reality (XR) environments can support multidisciplinary artistic creation through embodied interaction. While collaborative artistic practices such as music, dance, and theatre increasingly rely on digital tools, existing systems often remain discipline-specific and impose technical constraints that hinder creative exchange. This thesis explores how shared immersive environments can act as mediators between artistic modalities, enabling new forms of collaboration.

The research introduces S.P.A.R.K, a virtual reality prototype that enables asynchronous interaction between prerecorded dance movements and sound generation. Rather than relying on real-time motion capture, the system employs spatially distributed interaction objects that map movement data to audiovisual parameters via OSC, enabling integration with established tools such as Pure Data and Max/MSP. This asynchronous workflow allows artists to work independently while contributing to a coherent creative process.

The methodology follows a user-centered, iterative design approach involving professional and amateur artists through workshops and controlled user studies. Quantitative measures, including usability and creativity support questionnaires, are combined with qualitative analyses of user behavior and interviews to ground the design process in artists’ practices and collaborative needs. The research aims to contribute design insights for XR systems supporting artistic collaboration and multimodal interaction.
