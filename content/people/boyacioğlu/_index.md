## Thesis Title

Asymmetric Remote Collaboration for Physical Therapy and Implications on Rehabilitation/Movement Guidance

## Abstract

The project investigates asymmetric remote collaboration for physical rehabilitation, addressing the limitations of current single-user Extended Reality (XR) systems that lack personalized therapist oversight. While home-based automated feedback exists, it often fails to provide the specific guidance necessary to prevent injury or correct form effectively. This thesis proposes a mixed-reality framework where the therapist is virtually transported into the patient’s environment, aiming to restore the critical rapport and real-time guidance found in clinical settings. The primary objective is to determine optimal user representations and explore multimodal interaction techniques, specifically integrating multimodal feedback to facilitate motor learning. Unlike general remote collaboration, physiotherapy requires a distinct focus on the patient's biomechanics rather than the virtual environment itself. Through iterative prototyping and user studies with physiotherapists and patients, this work aims to derive design guidelines for remote guidance systems. Ultimately, the study evaluates the extent to which remote XR collaboration can effectively complement or substitute traditional in-person rehabilitation for patients with limited mobility.
