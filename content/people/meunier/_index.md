## Thesis Title

Augmenting Workspace Awareness with Passive BCI: From Monitoring to Enhancing Team Coordination 

## Abstract

While existing tools can support workspace awareness by monitoring observable actions, they have trouble capturing the underlying cognitive process essential for effective collaboration, particularly in situations of loose coupling or misalignment. This PhD project aims to investigate whether passive Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCIs) based on electroencephalography (EEG) can be used to identify neurophysiological markers that reflect different levels of collaborative coupling, ranging from loose to tight, as well as situations of mutual misunderstanding. The project will first explore the existence of such EEG markers in validated collaborative tasks. It will also evaluate the stability of these markers across different experimental paradigms, and across participants and time, known as sources of variability in EEG and BCI performance. If successful, the project will explore how these markers can be used as real-time neurofeedback to collaborators, to enhance their workspace awareness and reduce misalignment.
