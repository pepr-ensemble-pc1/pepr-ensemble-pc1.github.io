## Thesis Title

Remote Collaboration Strategies for Everyday Public Spaces 

## Abstract

My thesis explores how extended reality (XR) technologies transform social interaction, collaboration, and privacy when immersive systems are used in everyday, shared spaces. Rather than treating XR as an isolated virtual experience, my research examines XR as a situated practice embedded in environments such as offices, cafés, parks, libraries, and transportation settings, where social norms, spatial expectations, and bystander relationships already exist.

Using qualitative and design-oriented methods—including scenario-based workshops, interviews, and speculative design probes—I study how XR reconfigures visibility, attention, and social legibility among both users and non-users. One line of work focuses on collaborative XR, identifying tensions around trust, coordination, and legitimacy when immersive work becomes socially opaque or spatially disruptive. A second line investigates bystander privacy and privacy negotiation, examining how recording, sensing, and data capture in XR introduce new forms of implicit and explicit social negotiation in public and semi-public spaces.

Across these studies, the thesis develops a conceptual framework grounded in theories of social space to connect empirical findings with design implications. Overall, the research aims to inform the design of XR systems that support effective collaboration while enabling context-sensitive privacy negotiation and socially acceptable immersive interaction in everyday environments.
